## CLV is not one number

Most CLV conversations collapse the metric into a single dollar figure per guest, then chase that number. That is a workable abstraction for a coffee shop, where visit cadence is high and the basket is narrow. It is a misleading abstraction for hospitality.

Two reasons:

**Cadence is bimodal.** A casual-dining guest at the Tavern might come back every three weeks. A wedding-venue guest at Holden Beach Pavilion books once and you may never see them again. Treating both as a single CLV obscures who is actually retained.

**Baskets cross properties.** A guest who stayed at the Bald Head Island Club resort, ate at Kure Beach Grill the same week, and booked a spa session at Southport Waterfront Lodge is not three separate guests. They are one guest with one CLV, paying out across three P&Ls. Most reporting stacks do not roll that up.

## What a useful hospitality CLV looks like

We score CLV in three pieces, and we publish all three:

1. **Lifetime spend to date.** What this guest has paid across every property and every category.
2. **Projected 12-month spend.** Annual spend trended forward, discounted by churn probability.
3. **CLV concentration.** What share of total CLV the top 5% of guests represent.

In the demo dataset, the top 5% of guests at Cape Fear Hospitality Group hold about 31% of total CLV. That number alone reframes the marketing question. If you are sending the same email to 100% of guests every week, you are diluting attention away from the 5% who actually fund the business.

## What you do with it

A well-segmented CLV unlocks three programs that most operators leave on the table:

- **VIP concierge.** The top decile gets a dedicated host, suite preview access, and a different version of every campaign. This is the cheapest revenue you will ever recover.
- **Near-Platinum nudges.** Gold-tier guests within $150 of the Platinum threshold are not a campaign, they are a list. A bonus-points offer on the next visit pulls a measurable slice across the line.
- **Rising-star fast-track.** Silver guests showing 4x spend acceleration in the last 60 days are not Silver guests anymore. Welcoming them early is a retention move, not a marketing one.

## What it does not do

CLV is not a substitute for property-level reporting. The GM of the Tavern still needs to know what last Saturday looked like. CLV sits above that, in the corporate marketing and loyalty team, and answers a different question: *across all our properties, who matters most this year, and what should we do about them this week?*

When the answer to that question lives only in a spreadsheet a single analyst maintains, the program does not survive a personnel change. When it lives in a model that runs nightly against the live data, it does.
